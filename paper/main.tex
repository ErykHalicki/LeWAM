\begin{abstract}
World Action Models (WAMs) that predict future video latents and decode actions from those predictions have recently shown strong zero-shot generalization across robot embodiments~\citep{DreamZero}.
However, existing WAMs rely on large-scale compute infrastructure that is inaccessible to most researchers.
We present \textbf{LeWAM}, a compact World Action Model designed to run on consumer hardware.
LeWAM combines a frozen-pretrained V-JEPA2 video encoder with a lightweight flow-matching Diffusion Transformer (DiT) that predicts future patch embeddings in latent space, and an Inverse Dynamics Model (IDM) that decodes action chunks from the predicted transitions.
The separation of video prediction and action decoding allows the DiT to be pretrained on unlabeled internet video, and the IDM to be finetuned independently on small robot datasets.
We apply Sketched Isotropic Gaussian Regularization (SIGReg)~\citep{LeJEPA,LeWorldModel} to stabilize JEPA training and train on the SmolVLA community dataset~\citep{SmolVLA}.
Despite operating at a fraction of the parameter count of prior WAMs, LeWAM achieves competitive performance on [TODO: benchmark], demonstrating that strong video-conditioned action prediction does not require server-scale compute.
\end{abstract}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{World Action Models.}
\citet{DreamZero} introduced the World Action Model framework, showing that a video world model pretrained on internet-scale data can serve as a zero-shot policy across diverse robot embodiments when paired with an inverse dynamics model.
LeWAM adopts the same two-stage architecture but targets the low-compute regime.

\paragraph{Video self-supervised learning.}
V-JEPA~2\citep{vjepa2_1} and LeWorldModel~\citep{LeWorldModel} demonstrate that training predictors in the latent space of a video encoder is substantially more compute-efficient than pixel-space prediction.
We use the V-JEPA~2 ViT-L encoder as our visual backbone and propagate gradients through it end-to-end.

\paragraph{Stable JEPA training.}
Sketched Isotropic Gaussian Regularization (SIGReg), introduced in \citet{LeJEPA} and applied in \citet{LeWorldModel}, prevents representational collapse in JEPA-style models without requiring stop-gradient tricks.
We include SIGReg as an auxiliary loss throughout all training stages.

\paragraph{Diffusion Transformers and flow matching.}
DiT~\citep{DiT} established the transformer as a competitive backbone for diffusion models, replacing U-Nets with scalable attention blocks.
Flow matching~\citep{FlowMatching} offers a simpler and more stable training objective than DDPM-style diffusion by directly regressing the velocity field of a linear interpolation between noise and data.
We use flow matching with a DiT backbone, conditioning via cross-attention rather than AdaLN-Zero, following \citet{GenTron}.

\paragraph{3D positional encoding.}
3D-RoPE~\citep{ThreeDRoPE} extends Rotary Position Embeddings to temporal, height, and width axes, enabling length generalization beyond training resolution.
It is adopted in V-JEPA~2~\citep{vjepa2_1} and DreamZero~\citep{DreamZero}; we apply it to all spatial attention operations in both the DiT and IDM.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Overview}

LeWAM consists of three components: (1) a \textbf{visual encoder} that maps video frames to per-patch latent embeddings; (2) a \textbf{flow-matching DiT} that predicts future latent embeddings conditioned on past frames and a language instruction; and (3) an \textbf{Inverse Dynamics Model (IDM)} that decodes an action chunk from the current and predicted future embeddings.
Figure~\ref{fig:arch} illustrates the full system.

The DiT predicts future patch embeddings via flow matching, conditioned on past frames and a language instruction from a frozen T5Gemma encoder. The IDM cross-attends to current and predicted future patches to produce an action chunk.}

\subsection{Visual Encoder}

We use V-JEPA~2~\citep{vjepa2_1} ViT-L as our visual encoder. It operates on video clips of $T$ frames at resolution $H \times W$ with patch size $16 \times 16$ and tubelet size 2. For a $256 \times 256$ input, each frame produces a $16 \times 16$ grid of patch tokens, giving a total of $T \cdot H_p \cdot W_p$ tokens with embedding dimension $D_{\text{enc}} = 1024$. The encoder is initialized from the V-JEPA~2 pretrained weights and is \emph{not} frozen during training; gradients flow through it from both the DiT and IDM losses.

%---------------------------------------

\subsection{Language Encoder}

We use T5Gemma-S~\citep{T5Gemma} for its compact size (270M parameters) and text-only design, using only the encoder to obtain instruction embeddings. Language instructions are encoded into a sequence of $S$ token embeddings of dimension $D_{\text{lang}} = 512$. During training, the language conditioning is dropped with probability $p_{\text{drop}} = 0.15$ to enable classifier-free guidance at inference time \cite{DreamZero}. \subsection{Latent Predictor: Flow-Matching DiT}

Given the current patch embeddings $z_{\text{cur}} \in \mathbb{R}^{T H_p W_p \times D_{\text{enc}}}$, the DiT predicts the patch embeddings of $K$ future frames $z_{\text{fut}} \in \mathbb{R}^{K H_p W_p \times D_{\text{enc}}}$.

\paragraph{Flow matching objective.}
We define a linear interpolation between a noise sample $x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and the ground-truth future embeddings $z_{\text{fut}}$:
\begin{equation}
    x_t = (1 - t)\, x_0 + t\, z_{\text{fut}}, \quad t \sim \mathcal{U}[0, 1].
\end{equation}
The DiT is trained to predict the velocity field $v^* = z_{\text{fut}} - x_0$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}} = \mathbb{E}_{t,\, x_0}\left[\left\| v_\theta(x_t,\, t,\, z_{\text{cur}},\, l,\, s) - (z_{\text{fut}} - x_0) \right\|^2\right],
\end{equation}
where $l$ denotes the language token sequence and $s \in \mathbb{R}^{D_s}$ is the proprioceptive state.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
The DiT projects $x_t$ and $z_{\text{cur}}$ to a hidden dimension $D = 768$ via linear embedders. It applies $N_{\text{DiT}} = 12$ transformer blocks, each consisting of:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Self-attention} over noisy future tokens with 3D-RoPE positional encoding and adaLN-Zero conditioning on the timestep embedding $\tau(t)$.
    \item \textbf{Cross-attention} to past frame tokens (with 3D-RoPE), language tokens (no RoPE), and a state token (no RoPE), with all sources concatenated as keys and values.
    \item \textbf{MLP} with adaLN-Zero conditioning.
\end{enumerate}

At inference, $z_{\text{fut}}$ is recovered by integrating the learned velocity field from $t=0$ to $t=1$ using an ODE solver.

\subsection{Inverse Dynamics Model}

The IDM predicts an action chunk $\hat{a}_{1:K} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times D_a}$ (with $D_a = 7$) from the current and future patch embeddings.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
The IDM maintains $K$ learned action query tokens and applies $N_{\text{IDM}} = 6$ transformer blocks, each consisting of:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Cross-attention} from action queries to current frame tokens and future frame tokens (both with 3D-RoPE), and to a state token.
    \item \textbf{Self-attention} over action queries (bidirectional, no positional encoding).
    \item \textbf{MLP}.
\end{enumerate}
The IDM uses standard LayerNorm throughout (no adaLN). The final action token representations are projected to $D_a$ via a linear head.

\paragraph{Training.}
The IDM is supervised with an MSE loss on the action chunk:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{IDM}} = \left\| \hat{a}_{1:K} - a_{1:K}^* \right\|^2.
\end{equation}
To bridge the train/test gap between ground-truth and predicted future embeddings, we use \textbf{teacher forcing}: with probability $p_{\text{tf}}$, the IDM is conditioned on the ground-truth future patches; otherwise on embeddings generated by the DiT via ODE solving. \subsection{Training Objectives}

The combined training loss is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}} + \lambda_{\text{IDM}}\, \mathcal{L}_{\text{IDM}} + \lambda_{\text{SIGReg}}\, \mathcal{L}_{\text{SIGReg}}(Z),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{SIGReg}}$ is the Sketched Isotropic Gaussian Regularization loss~\citep{LeJEPA} applied to the encoder output $Z$ to prevent representational collapse. During video-only pretraining (no action labels), the IDM term is dropped.

%---------------------------------------

\subsection{Data}

\paragraph{Video pretraining.}
Before finetuning on robot data, we pretrain the DiT and the V-JEPA~2 encoder jointly on Something-Something v2 (SSv2)~\citep{SomethingSomething}, a large-scale dataset of 220k crowd-sourced videos depicting humans performing 174 fine-grained manipulation categories (\emph{e.g.}\ ``pushing something from left to right'', ``pouring something into something'').
SSv2 is particularly well-suited for this purpose because its clips are action-centric, object-interaction-heavy, and temporally causal in a way that mirrors the structure of robot manipulation, unlike scene-centric datasets such as Kinetics.
Each clip is annotated with a natural-language action label, which we use as the language conditioning signal for the DiT, allowing the text and vision encoders to align on grounded, manipulation-relevant concepts before any robot data is seen.
During this stage the language encoder (T5Gemma-S) remains frozen and only the DiT and V-JEPA~2 encoder weights are updated.
The training objective is $\mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}} + \lambda_{\text{SIGReg}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{SIGReg}}$ (no IDM term, as SSv2 carries no action labels).
